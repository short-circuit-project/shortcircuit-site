Five reads on the mid-market AI opportunity — and the bet I'm making about your practice.

If you're reading this, you're one of maybe four people who ever will. I built it for the role I'm applying for, and for the practice you're building. Five short points I think are true about where the SMB market is heading, each one paired with a sharpening from Claude. One pointed question at the end.

Richie Barden • Applicant, Managing Consultant • Built with Claude
1 Point One

Management change is the real opportunity at SMBs — and SMBs haven't built that muscle.

R Richie

Small and mid-sized businesses have survived the last two decades without building a real change management practice. They've gotten by because they hired people who already knew how things worked. The knowing was the system. That isn't change management — that's continuity. And continuity is about to meet a transition.

C Claude — sharpening

The reason this is a consulting opportunity and not an HR one: the replacement hire doesn't fix it. The operator pipeline you'd normally draw from is the same pipeline that's exiting. What has to replace the retiring operator is structural — documented standard work, explicit decision rights, AI-augmented process. Practices that can deliver that reconstructive work — not just the AI layer on top — own this decade.

2 Point Two

Boomers are concentrated in leadership and working later than any prior generation. The skill gap is coming.

R Richie

Boomers hold a disproportionate share of SMB leadership seats. They're also working longer than any generation before them. Both trends have been masking the same problem: the org chart has been absorbing institutional knowledge into a small number of senior heads for twenty-plus years, and when those heads exit — slowly, and then all at once — the skill gap is going to be real.

C Claude — sharpening

The sharper version: there is no 45-year-old bench in most of these businesses. The usual fix — outside hires — doesn't work in companies that run on relationship, operator judgment, and tacit process. Which means the only working answer is to build systems that lower the judgment burden on the replacement. That is an AI-plus-change-management problem, and it is the central question in Richie's thesis. Answer it well and you've defined the decade.

3 Point Three

AI adoption over the next five years is make-or-break for these generational transitions.

R Richie

The five-year window decides whether the transition is a cliff or a bridge. The companies that figure out how to absorb AI while they still have operators around to teach it what "right" looks like will be fine. The companies that try to absorb AI after the operators are gone are going to struggle — and by then the skill gap is compounding in both directions.

C Claude — sharpening

Note the implied sequencing — operator-to-AI knowledge transfer is a finite-window opportunity, not an evergreen one. Every quarter of delay compounds against the client. The consulting implication: the highest-leverage SMB engagement available right now is the one that captures institutional knowledge into AI-readable structure before the operator retires. That's a new deliverable category. I don't think the market has fully priced it in yet.

4 Point Four

Legacy systems that can't talk to each other can now be bridged cheaply. That changes the shape of the work.

R Richie

Most SMBs have old systems and old ways of doing things. The systems don't talk to each other natively, so humans are the integration layer — copying numbers from one screen to another. You don't need to replace the CRM or the ERP. You just need to glue them together. Automations that never worked inside a legacy system can now be bridged on top of it.

C Claude — sharpening

The second-order effect matters more than the first. If AI-as-integration-layer works, it erodes the case for big ERP replacement projects that have anchored mid-market IT spend for twenty years. That's a real portfolio shift for every consulting practice that serves this segment. Decision Intelligence practices that lean into integration-as-a-service take share from traditional digital transformation practices still selling rip-and-replace. The disruption isn't just to the client — it's to the consultants too.

5 Point Five

High-leverage strategic work gets back-burnered forever. AI finally unlocks it at SMB scale.

R Richie

Every SMB leader has a mental list of work that should get done but never does: forecasting, market research, pricing analysis, competitive intelligence. It doesn't get done because it doesn't ship product to a customer — so it gets pushed to next week, forever. The urgent eats the important. AI compresses that work from weeks to hours, and for the first time it's actually feasible at SMB scale.

C Claude — sharpening

The honest read: strategic work was never unavailable to SMBs for analytical reasons. It was unavailable for labor reasons. AI doesn't invent new strategy work — it changes the economics of who can afford to do it. That is a real redistribution event. The consulting opportunity is to be the practice that brings the work large enterprises have been doing for forty years into companies that couldn't previously pencil it. Not a transformation. A democratization.

? One question for you

If AI capability is increasingly commodity, the differentiator for a practice like yours becomes the ability to pair it with credible change management delivery. Is that already the internal conversation at BRG — or is it still getting framed as a tooling play?

I'd value the read from inside the practice. The five points above are where I'd start on a mid-market engagement if I sat at your table — and I'd want to know how close or far that is from how you're already framing it. Reply and tell me I'm wrong. Or tell me I'm close. That's the conversation I'd want to have first.
